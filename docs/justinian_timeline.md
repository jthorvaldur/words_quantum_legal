## The Julian Calendar (45 BC)

In 45 BC, Julius Caesar replaced the older Roman calendar with a
solar-based system of 365.25 days. This was the first act of temporal
standardization under a single imperial authority.

### What Changed

| Before | After |
|--------|-------|
| Lunar months, variable lengths | Fixed solar months |
| Local timekeeping, regional variation | Empire-wide uniform calendar |
| Priests controlled intercalation | State-mandated leap years |
| Time was organic, agricultural | Time became administrative |

### The Significance

The Julian Calendar removed time from local, organic measurement and placed
it under centralized Roman control. Whoever controls the calendar controls:

- **When debts are due** (commerce)
- **When taxes are assessed** (revenue)
- **When contracts expire** (law)
- **When religious observances occur** (spiritual authority)
- **When wars begin and end** (military power)

Time is not neutral. It is jurisdictional. By standardizing it, Caesar made
every date in the empire a Roman date -- an imperial instrument.

## Anno Domini (525 AD)

In 525 AD, the monk Dionysius Exiguus invented the Anno Domini dating
system, replacing the Diocletian era (which counted from the accession of
the Roman emperor Diocletian in 284 AD).

### The Reframe

| Before | After |
|--------|-------|
| Years counted from emperor's accession | Years counted from Christ's birth |
| Time anchored to living political power | Time anchored to theological event |
| Each emperor resets the count | One continuous count, Church-controlled |
| Secular authority over dates | Ecclesiastical authority over dates |

### The Effect

All of history -- past, present, and future -- was reframed under Church
authority. Every date you write (2026 AD) is an assertion of the Church's
temporal framework. The year does not measure astronomical cycles alone; it
measures position relative to a theological claim.

The phrase "Anno Domini" means "In the Year of Our Lord." Every dated
document implicitly acknowledges this lordship. The calendar is a contract.

## The Corpus Juris Civilis (530-565 AD)

Emperor Justinian I commissioned the most consequential legal codification
in Western history. Compiled between 530 and 565 AD by Tribonian and a
commission of jurists, the Corpus Juris Civilis became the foundation of
all European civil law and, through it, the modern legal system.

### The Five Components

#### 1. The Digest (Pandects) -- 533 AD

Fifty books of legal opinions extracted from classical Roman jurists.
**Book XIV is critical**: it contains the maritime law provisions,
including Title I: "Concerning The Action Against The Owner Of A Ship."

This is where admiralty jurisdiction enters the codified legal system.
The ship-owner's liability, the master's authority, the cargo's status --
all the concepts that would later be applied to PERSONS (vessels) in
commercial courts.

#### 2. The Institutes -- 533 AD

A textbook for training lawyers. It defined the categories:

- **Persons** (not men and women -- legal entities)
- **Things** (property, including people classified as property)
- **Actions** (legal proceedings -- how the system processes its cargo)

The Institutes taught generations of lawyers to think in terms of persons,
things, and actions -- not living men, land, and rights.

#### 3. The Codex -- 534 AD

A compilation of imperial constitutions (decrees). The emperor IS the law.
His word creates obligation. The Codex established the principle that
legislation flows downward from supreme authority -- the model for every
statute, code, and regulation since.

#### 4. The Novellae -- 534-565 AD

New laws issued by Justinian after the Codex. The emperor makes law by
decree, in real time, with no requirement for consent of the governed.
This is the ancestor of executive orders and administrative regulations.

#### 5. The GLOSSA -- The Interpretive Overlay

The most consequential component for understanding the modern deception.

The GLOSSA is the all-uppercase "translation" layer that sits on top of
the actual law and redefines it. Medieval glossators (legal commentators)
wrote annotations in the margins of the Corpus Juris Civilis in a
standardized uppercase form. These glosses became authoritative -- in many
cases MORE authoritative than the original text.

The GLOSSA is the ancestor of DOG-LATIN.

## Imperial Dating and the Indiction Cycle (537 AD)

### The Emperor's Name on All Dates

In 537 AD, Justinian issued Novella 47, requiring that all legal documents
include the emperor's name and regnal year in their dating. This was not
administrative convenience. It was jurisdictional capture.

Every date on every document became an assertion of imperial authority. The
document exists within the emperor's time. The emperor's time IS the
jurisdiction.

### The Indiction Cycle

The indiction was a 15-year tax assessment cycle, originally instituted by
Diocletian (297 AD) and formalized by Justinian as a primary temporal
framework.

| Year in Cycle | Function |
|---------------|----------|
| Year 1-5 | Assessment period: property and persons cataloged |
| Year 6-10 | Collection period: obligations extracted |
| Year 11-15 | Review and reassessment |
| Year 1 (restart) | New cycle: fresh assessment of everything |

The indiction cycle made every date relative to the tax cycle. Time itself
became a measurement of your position within the extraction framework. You
do not merely exist in a year; you exist at a specific point in a 15-year
cycle of assessment, collection, and reassessment.

Modern equivalents: fiscal years, tax years, assessment periods, quarterly
reporting cycles. The indiction never went away. It was absorbed into the
commercial calendar.

## Abolition of the Consulship (541 AD)

In 541 AD, Justinian abolished the Roman consulship -- the last surviving
institution that provided a non-imperial dating reference.

### Before Abolition

Dates could be expressed as: "In the consulship of Flavius Basilius" --
referencing a specific person holding a republican office.

### After Abolition

All dates could only reference: the emperor, the indiction cycle, or the
Anno Domini system. Every non-imperial temporal anchor was removed.

This completed the enclosure of time. There was no longer any way to date a
document without invoking either:
- Imperial authority (emperor's name/regnal year)
- Ecclesiastical authority (Anno Domini)
- Commercial authority (indiction/tax cycle)

Time became a three-jurisdiction instrument: imperial, ecclesiastical,
commercial. Sound familiar?

## The Gregorian Calendar Reform (1582 AD)

In 1582, Pope Gregory XIII issued the papal bull *Inter gravissimas*,
reforming the Julian Calendar by:

1. **Deleting 10 days** (October 4, 1582 was followed by October 15, 1582)
2. **Modifying the leap year rule** (century years divisible by 400 only)
3. **Reasserting Church control over the calendar**

### The Jurisdictional Act

The Pope literally deleted days from existence. Ten days that people lived
through, worked through, contracted through -- erased by papal decree.

| Country | Date Adopted | Days Lost |
|---------|-------------|-----------|
| Catholic Europe (Spain, Portugal, Italy) | 1582 | 10 days |
| France | 1582 | 10 days |
| Protestant German states | 1700 | 10 days |
| Great Britain and colonies | 1752 | 11 days |
| Russia | 1918 | 13 days |
| Greece | 1923 | 13 days |

Protestant nations resisted for over a century -- not because the astronomy
was wrong, but because accepting the Gregorian Calendar meant accepting
papal authority over time. Britain held out for 170 years.

When Britain finally adopted the Gregorian Calendar in 1752, the American
colonies were included. Every date in American law after September 14, 1752
is a Gregorian date -- a papal date. The separation of Church and State
does not extend to the calendar.

### The Compounding Effect

By 1582, the Gregorian reform layered atop:
- The Julian standardization (imperial time)
- The Anno Domini framework (ecclesiastical time)
- The indiction cycle (commercial time)
- The GLOSSA overlay (symbolic time)

Every date is now simultaneously:
- An imperial measurement (inherited from Rome)
- A theological assertion (Anno Domini)
- A commercial position (within fiscal/tax cycles)
- A symbolic designation (written in DOG-LATIN on legal documents)
